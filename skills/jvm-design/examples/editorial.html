The
Agentic
Enterprise.

The org chart is dissolving. In its place: a living mesh of human judgement and autonomous software, negotiating goals in real time. We spent six months inside the companies rebuilding themselves around it — and found a new operating model already taking hold.

For thirty years, the dream of digital business was the workflow — a tidy sequence of steps, each one automated, measured, and improved. The agentic enterprise breaks that frame entirely. Instead of a pipeline, picture a marketplace: dozens of autonomous agents bidding on objectives, escalating only the trade-offs that genuinely require a human verdict. The work no longer flows. It negotiates.

Walk into the operations centre of a Berlin-based logistics company we visited last winter and you will not see dashboards. You will see goals. A wall of them, written in plain language: reduce return rate in DACH by 4% without compromising delivery promise. Beneath each goal is a quiet swarm of agents — pricing, routing, customer comms, supplier negotiation — working the problem in parallel, surfacing tensions, and handing the sharpest ones to the four humans on duty.

The end of the workflow

The workflow assumed that work could be specified in advance. The agentic enterprise assumes only the outcome can be specified — the path is rediscovered every minute. This is more than a technical upgrade. It is a categorical shift in what an organisation is.

We stopped writing processes.
We started writing intentions. — Helena Brand, COO, Vossloh Logistik

From tools to teammates

Software used to wait for instructions. Agents do not. They open tickets, draft contracts, schedule calls, refuse tasks they consider out of scope, and ask their human counterparts for clarification when goals collide. The interface metaphor of the last decade — a button you press — is being replaced by something closer to a conversation across a desk.

Three behaviours mark the shift:

  • Initiative. Agents propose work, not just execute it.
  • Memory. They carry context across sessions, projects, and quarters.
  • Accountability. Every decision is signed, traced, and reviewable.

The negotiation layer

The most underappreciated invention of the agentic enterprise is not the agent itself, but the negotiation layer beneath it — a shared protocol through which agents declare goals, expose constraints, and broker compromises. Companies that have built this layer well move with the coherence of a single mind. Companies that have not, drown in contradiction.

The org chart was a map of who reports to whom.
The mesh is a map of who negotiates with whom.

A new managerial craft

Managing an agent team is unlike managing a human one, but not in the ways we expected. The hard part is not delegation — agents accept work gladly. The hard part is intention: writing goals that are precise enough to act on and humane enough to live with. The new managerial craft is, essentially, editorial.

At the agencies and brands we work with, the role we increasingly recognise is the intention designer — half strategist, half writer, wholly accountable for the goals the mesh pursues on the company's behalf.

What we learned

The agentic enterprise is not a future state. It is an operating model already in production at the most curious companies in Europe. It will not arrive as a vendor announcement. It will arrive as a quiet shift in the meeting you are in next week — the moment someone says, let's ask the agent to take a first pass, and no one looks surprised.

That is the moment the org chart dissolves. What replaces it is more alive, more responsive, and — if we design it well — more deeply human than the bureaucracy we are leaving behind.
